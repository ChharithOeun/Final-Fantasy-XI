# Character Creation

> The first 90 seconds. Make them count.

Character creation is the player's first contact with FFXI Demoncore. Before they fight a goblin, before they meet an NPC, before they hear the soundtrack — they spend their first long, quiet moment on this screen. If it doesn't feel like a film opening, the rest of the game is fighting uphill.

## What it is NOT

- A static menu of dropdowns.
- A spreadsheet of stats with a small character preview in the corner.
- A "press X to randomize" carousel.

That's how 2002 did it. We're not doing that.

## What it is

A 90-second guided cinematic. The player wakes up. They are a presence in Vana'diel, not yet defined. The camera is on them. As they make choices — race, face, hair, gear, jobs, voice — they become themselves on screen. When they hit "Begin," the camera lifts off and the opening cinematic of their starting nation rolls.

## Shot grammar

Treat the screen as the opening shot of a film. The character creator IS the opening shot. Specifically:

- **Establishing**: dawn light through a window onto an empty room. We hear the city outside (Bastok forge work / Sandy bell / Windy chimes). Camera is at chest height, slightly low angle, locked off.
- **Reveal**: as the player picks a race, the silhouette materializes from particle dust into the empty space the camera is framed on. No menu transitions — the character builds itself from light.
- **Push in**: each customization step (face, hair, eyes) the camera pushes in incrementally. By the time they're picking gear, we're at a 3/4 portrait shot. The lens compresses appropriately (35mm wide → 50mm portrait → 85mm hero close).
- **Voice preview**: the player picks their voice; the character speaks one line in it. The line is procedurally generated by Higgs Audio off a short reference seed and reflects their starting nation ("...alright, let's see what's out there." for Bastok; "Today is mine." for Sandy; etc.). Player can re-roll the voice clip.
- **Music**: a slow, building cue from the regenerated soundtrack underneath the customization. Different cue per starting nation. No silence; never silence.
- **The Begin moment**: when they hit Begin, the music drops to a single sustained note. Camera lifts up and away from the character. Cut to black. Cut to the opening cinematic.

## Layout

The player isn't navigating menus alone in the dark. The customization controls live as a glass panel overlay on the right third of the screen. The character is the center. The panel is unobtrusive (60% opacity, dark frame), respects the cinematic tone.

Controls grouped by the order they matter:

1. **Nation** — pick first; everything else inherits from this (starting zone, opening cinematic, default voice tone, signature attire). One of three: Bastok, San d'Oria, Windurst. Plus a fourth, **Whitegate**, unlocked for veteran accounts.
2. **Race** — Hume / Elvaan / Tarutaru / Mithra / Galka. Visual side-by-side preview shows all five at once before commit. The chosen race materializes; the others fade.
3. **Sub-race / face / hair / eyes / skin** — per-race variants, original FFXI presets present plus a "Modern" set we add (more variation, less 2002-stylized).
4. **Gear** — starter set (race-appropriate). Cosmetic only at this stage; jobs come at level 1.
5. **Voice** — Higgs Audio voice bank. Three pre-cloned voice anchors per race, plus a "Custom" option that lets the player record a 30-second reference (saved to their account voice bank for in-game lines later — you can hear yourself talk to NPCs).
6. **Name** — last because it commits everything. Once entered, the toon is reserved server-side.
7. **Begin** — the button is offset, large, mostly empty space around it. Pressing it is a moment.

## Race redesigns (FFXI Demoncore variants)

Original FFXI character meshes get extracted, upscaled, then refit to modern PBR materials per the asset pipeline. Specific per-race tweaks for Demoncore:

| Race | Original | Demoncore variant |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **Hume** | Default human, fine | Improved face topology, better hair shaders, more subdivisions on cloth — let the mesh hold up at film distance |
| **Elvaan** | Tall, elongated proportions | Elongated proportions preserved; eye and ear meshes redone for closer-camera readability |
| **Tarutaru** | Stylized chibi | Style preserved (we're not making them realistic — the aesthetic is the point); shaders modernized for cuter expressions |
| **Mithra** | Catgirl | Fur shading via UE5 Groom, modern face rig with proper expressions, ears + tail use KawaiiPhysics |
| **Galka** | Tall, broad, **tail** | **Tail removed.** User preference. Galka without tails reads cleaner as a heroic frame. Tail anchor bone stays in skeleton (for future modders), just isn't rendered or animated. Otherwise broader shoulders, more fur detail, bigger silhouette |

The galka tail removal is implemented at the asset-extraction step: when we pull galka_male.dat / galka_female.dat through the extract pipeline, the resulting mesh has a tail bone group; we delete that bone group's vertex assignments and re-export. Animation never targets it. Done.

## Music + sound

Each starting nation has its theme cue from the regenerated ACE-Step soundtrack. The cue is split into intro/loop/outro stems so it can sit under any-length customization session. When the player commits, the cue resolves to its outro and bridges into the opening cinematic music.

Sound design beats:
- City ambience (forge, bells, chimes) loops at low volume throughout
- Each customization choice (race pick, hair pick) plays a soft confirmation chime, race-specific
- The voice preview plays at full mix; we ducking-attenuate the music underneath
- Begin button hover plays a sustained low note; click drops everything to silence + the music single-note sustain

## Technical implementation

- **Engine**: UE5.7 Sequencer manages the cinematic timeline; a state machine in C++/Blueprint handles which Sequencer tracks play during which customization step.
- **Character rig**: MetaHuman base rig for hero (close-up) renders, original FFXI mesh for in-game gameplay. Switch happens at "Begin" — the MetaHuman version is the one in cutscenes thereafter.
- **Voice clone**: when player records a custom 30-second reference, it goes through Higgs Audio's voice-bank registration on the chharbot server. Their account gets a new voice bank ID. NPCs that talk *to* this character will use TTS that calls them by name in-character.
- **Lighting**: a single dynamic key light (warm, animated dawn movement), a fill at 1/4 key intensity (cool), a rim from behind. The light setup IS the camera-direction grammar — we can change the entire scene mood by remapping these three lights.
- **Camera**: the orbit-and-push-in path is a Sequencer Camera Anim 2 asset that the customization-state machine triggers per step. No procedural camera math; pre-authored moves.

## Anti-frustration rules

The screen is reverent but it shouldn't take away player agency:

- **Skip cinematic** option (disabled on first launch only — first impression rules). After that, return players can skip.
- **Re-roll** every step freely; nothing commits until the Begin button.
- **Compare** mode: side-by-side previews of any two configurations.
- **Preset import**: a player who's made the same character on another server can paste a JSON preset and skip ahead to "Name" + "Begin."
- **The screen reads at 4K and at 1080p** — don't let the cinematic framing punish low-res monitors.

## What it borrows from real cinematic openings

A few specific references the team should look at when iterating:

- **The Last of Us II** main menu — single character in a single light, the room itself is the menu.
- **Death Stranding** introduction — silence, pace, weight. We don't move fast in customization for the same reason that movie doesn't move fast in its opening.
- **The Mandalorian** virtual production grammar — see `CINEMATICS.md` for how that applies. Character creation is a lit sound stage in our case.

## Build order

1. **Static slate.** One character (Hume Bastok), MetaHuman rig, one light setup, one cue. End-to-end, no customization yet — just prove the slate looks right.
2. **Race-pick loop.** Add the side-by-side preview, the materialize-from-particles transition, the per-race chime.
3. **Customization controls.** Glass panel UI, push-in camera per step.
4. **Voice integration.** Higgs Audio preview, custom-record path, full voice bank registration.
5. **Begin transition.** The lift-out, music drop, cut-to-cinematic. Hand off to the per-nation opening movie.
6. **All five races + their variants land.** Galka without tail in this pass.

By step 5 the slate is shippable. Steps 6+ are content.

# CHARACTER_CREATION — Naming Rules Patch

Append to `CHARACTER_CREATION.md` Section 6 (Name) with these specifics. The original doc said "Once entered, the toon is reserved server-side." This is the full spec.

## Names are server-wide unique, across all entity types

A name claimed by ANY entity in the world is unavailable to new characters. The check covers:

- **Live player characters** — current and historical (a deleted character's name stays reserved for 90 real days)
- **Fomor characters** — any fomor that exists in the spawn pool (including dormant ones during day cycles)
- **NPCs** — every named civilian, shopkeeper, guild master, quest-giver, magistrate, etc.
- **Notorious Monsters (NMs)** — every named NM, current and historical
- **Bosses** — every named boss across all expansions
- **Beastmen of name** — beastmen NPCs who have proper names (Yagudo Avatar, Quadav Stoneworker, etc.)

This is intentionally exhaustive. There is no Aldo, no Lion, no Curilla, no Bahamut, no Zeid, no Maat name allowed for new players. The Vana'diel canon is preserved.

## Name validation rules

When the player tries to commit a name:

1. **Length check**: 3-15 characters
2. **Character set**: A-Z, a-z, 0-9, hyphen, apostrophe (FFXI-traditional permitted set). No spaces. No special characters.
3. **Format check**: Must START with a letter. Cannot end with hyphen or apostrophe.
4. **Profanity filter**: Server-side regex + LLM-assisted classifier (chharbot is the moderator) checks against:
   - English profanity in word and l33t-spoke variants
   - Slurs in any major language
   - Sexual content references
   - Real-world political/religious figures used disparagingly
   - The classifier handles edge cases the regex misses
5. **Reserved-name check**: hits the global name table (players + fomors + NPCs + NMs + bosses)
6. **Confusable check**: names that are visually too similar to staff/admin accounts or famous NPCs are rejected (e.g., "Aldoo", "L1on", "Bahamuth")
7. **Trademark check**: rejects obvious brand names (Disney, Sony, etc.)

Failure on any check returns a specific error so the player knows what to fix:

- "That name is already in use by [entity type]." (without revealing which specific entity, to prevent information leaks)
- "That name violates community standards." (catch-all for profanity/slur)
- "That name is too similar to an existing character." (confusable case)

## Reservation

Once committed, the name is reserved permanently to that account. The reservation persists:

- Across character deletes (90 real-day grace period before name returns to pool — so a player who deletes by accident has time to recreate)
- Across the toon-becoming-fomor lifecycle (the name stays with the fomor as long as the fomor exists; if the fomor is killed and not respawned for 30 real days, the name returns to the pool)
- Across server merges (very unlikely in our private-server context, but spec'd just in case)

## Edge case: fomor renaming

A specific edge case: a player whose character becomes a fomor still has their name on the fomor. If that player wants to make a NEW character, they can't reuse that name because their *own* fomor is still using it. This is intentional — the fomor IS that name, in the world.

If the player wants their name back, they have to:

1. Hunt and kill their own fomor
2. Wait the 30-day reservation cooldown
3. Re-create at that point

Or: pick a new name. Most players pick the new name. The old name continues as a wraith identity.

## Profanity filter implementation

Two layers:

### Layer 1: regex blocklist

Maintained as `assets/name_blocklist.txt` in the FFXI-Demoncore project. Standard wordlists, l33t variants, common substitution patterns. Conservative — better to false-reject and let a player retry than allow something offensive.

### Layer 2: chharbot LLM classifier

For names that pass the regex but feel suspicious, chharbot's MCP receives the name + claims:

```python
# In moderation.py, called from character creation
def classify_name(name: str) -> dict:
    # Check 1: is the name pronounceable / typo-realistic?
    # Check 2: any subliminal offensive content?
    # Check 3: any reference to public figures used disparagingly?
    # Returns: {"approve": bool, "reason": str, "confidence": float}
```

If the classifier returns `approve=False` with confidence > 0.7, name is rejected. Below that confidence, the name passes but is logged for human review (a low-cost background sweep that flags retroactive removals if needed).

### Appeals

A player whose name is rejected by the LLM classifier can appeal:

- Click "Appeal" in the rejection dialog
- Brief text box explaining why the name is appropriate
- Goes to chharbot's moderation queue
- Resolved within 24 real hours
- Approved appeals override the classifier decision and the name is reserved

This handles the case of culturally-specific names (Khmer, Tagalog, Korean, etc.) that might trigger an over-aggressive classifier. Chharbot is configured to be lenient on cultural names; appeals approve almost all legitimate cases.

## Name display

In-game UI rendering:

- **Player names**: white text by default; tinted by faction (gold for nation-citizen, red for outlaw, purple for fomor-targeted player)
- **NPC names**: standard white
- **NM names**: blue
- **Boss names**: red
- **Fomor names**: bright purple (always visible — fomor identity is public)

Above-head display can be toggled per-class in player settings (some prefer to hide ambient NPC names for cleaner battlefields).

## What this enables

The name system reinforces the persistent-world feel:

- Every name in the world is unique. When you hear about "Tarutaru-name X," you know exactly who that is.
- A famous name (a server-first kill, a long-lived high-tier outlaw, a high-bounty fomor) becomes lore. Other players reference them.
- Player-fomor recursion creates a chain of *named* identities — the fomor has the original player's name, and the next player who lives wearing that fomor's gear can be referenced as "the one who beat <fomor name>."

Names are weight-bearing in Demoncore. The naming system enforces that.
